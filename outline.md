## Slide 2: The Economic Trilemma & Mackenroth’s Theorem

- **The Fundamental Constraint:** The "Pension Trilemma."
  - 1. Contribution Rate (_Beitragssatz_): Capped politically (approx. 20-22%).
  - 2. Replacement Rate (_Rentenniveau_): Targeted floor (48%).
  - 3. Retirement Age: Fixed statutory limits.
  - _Constraint:_ You can only fix two; the third acts as the release valve.
- **Theoretical Basis:** _Mackenroth’s Theorem (1952)_:
  - "All social expenditure must always be paid out of the current national product."
  - Capital funding vs. PAYG is a second-order distribution question; the aggregate output ($Y$) is the constraint.

> **Speaker Notes:** Start strong with theory. Mackenroth argues that whether you have a funded system or PAYG, the goods consumed by retirees must be produced by the _current_ workforce. [cite_start]Compare this to the Swiss presentation, which highlights that demographics challenge _labor market_ productivity regardless of the funding mechanism[cite: 148]. Germany is currently trying to "break" the trilemma by introducing tax subsidies to hold both rates constant.

## Slide 3: Institutional Design – The Dominant First Pillar

- **Structure:**
  - **Pillar 1 (GRV):** Mandatory PAYG. Covers ~90% of workforce. Provides ~75% of old-age income.
  - **Pillar 2 (bAV):** Voluntary/Sectoral.
  - **Pillar 3 (Private):** "Riester" (subsidized) – largely failed due to complexity/costs.
- [cite_start]**Comparison:** unlike the Swiss "Three Pillar" balance [cite: 338] or the Dutch model, Germany is historically "Mono-Pillar" dependent.
- **Financing:**
  - Contributions (Employer/Employee split 50/50).
  - **Federal Subsidy (_Bundeszuschuss_):** Covers ~30% of expenditures (non-insurance benefits).

> [cite_start]**Speaker Notes:** Contrast this immediately with the Swiss system shown in your course material, where the 2nd Pillar is mandatory and fully funded[cite: 424]. In Germany, Pillar 1 is the "mammoth." This makes the system highly sensitive to demographic shifts because there is less risk diversification through capital markets (until now).

## Slide 4: Microeconomics of the "Points System" (_Entgeltpunkte_)

- **The Logic:** "Points," not "Final Salary."
- **Accumulation Mechanism:**
  - $$EP_t = \frac{\text{Individual Gross Income}_t}{\text{Average Gross Income}_t}$$
  - 1 Year at Average Earnings = 1.0 Point.
- **Incentive Structure (The Equivalence Principle):**
  - Strict linearity ($Contribution \approx Benefit$).
  - **Economic Effect:** Reduces the "implicit tax" character of contributions. Workers perceive contributions as "deferred wages," theoretically minimizing labor supply distortions.

> **Speaker Notes:** This is crucial economic design. Unlike redistributive systems (e.g., UK/US), Germany is status-preserving (_Lebensstandardsicherung_). If you were poor in working life, you are poor in retirement. [cite_start]This strict link is why the German system has high acceptance but struggles with old-age poverty, similar to the regional poverty variance seen in Switzerland[cite: 616].

## Slide 5: The Pension Formula (The Mechanics)

- **The Equation:**
  - $$Pension_{Monthly} = \sum EP \times ZF \times RAF \times AR$$
- **Variables:**
  - **$\sum EP$:** Lifetime Earnings Points.
  - **$ZF$ (Access Factor):** Actuarial neutrality mechanism.
    - Retiring early = 0.3% permanent reduction per month.
  - **$RAF$:** Pension Type (1.0 = Old Age).
  - **$AR$ (Current Pension Value):** The "Exchange Rate" of points to Euros.

> **Speaker Notes:** Focus on $ZF$. This is an attempt at _actuarial neutrality_. If a specific individual retires early, they draw the pension for longer. The 0.3% cut attempts to make the _Present Value (PV)_ of the pension stream identical regardless of retirement age.

---

## Slide 6: The Adjustment Mechanism (Sustainability Factor)

- **Dynamic Indexation:** Pensions follow wages ($AR$ grows with $Wages$).
- **The Dampener ($\alpha$): The Sustainability Factor (2004).**
  - $$Adjustment_t \propto WageGrowth_t \times \left( 1 - \alpha \cdot \frac{DependencyRatio_t}{DependencyRatio_{t-1}} \right)$$
- **Function:**
  - Shifts demographic risk from contributors (workers) to retirees.
  - If the ratio of pensioners/workers rises, pension increases act as a lag on wage growth.

> [cite_start]**Speaker Notes:** This is Germany's version of the Japanese "Macroeconomic Slide"[cite: 1014]. [cite_start]While Japan uses the slide to automatically reduce benefit levels based on life expectancy and workforce decline[cite: 1015], Germany uses the Sustainability Factor to dampen the _increase_. It is a soft brake, whereas Japan’s is a hard brake.

## Slide 7: The Demographic Shock (Externalities)

- **The Data:**
  - **Baby Boomer Exit:** Cohorts 1955–1969 retiring between 2020–2035.
  - **Old Age Dependency Ratio (OADR):** Rising from ~35 to ~50.
- **Labor Market Implications:**
  - Shrinking labor supply ($L$) $\rightarrow$ Potential drag on GDP Growth ($g$).
  - With $r > g$ (Piketty’s inequality), reliance on wage-based PAYG becomes regressive/inefficient compared to capital funding.

> [cite_start]**Speaker Notes:** Use the data from the Swiss presentation as a proxy for the trend in Western Europe: "Each successive generation is smaller than that of its parents"[cite: 117]. Germany faces this same "inverted pyramid."

---

## Slide 8: Labor Supply & Retirement Age

- **Policy Response:** "Rente mit 67" (2007 Reform).
  - Gradual increase of Statutory Retirement Age (SRA) to 67 by 2031.
- **The "Rente mit 63" Anomaly (2014):**
  - Allowed tax-free early retirement for long contributors.
  - **Economic Critique:** Deadweight loss. Subsidized the exit of highly productive, skilled labor during a shortage.
- **Effective vs. Statutory Age:**
  - Gap remains, though closing.

> **Speaker Notes:** Discuss _Labor Force Participation (LFP)_. [cite_start]Just like Japan is trying to encourage seniors to stay in the labor force to mitigate shrinkage[cite: 1056], Germany raised the age. However, "Rente mit 63" was a political gift that contradicted the economic necessity.

## Slide 9: The "Standard Pensioner" & Replacement Rates

- **Standard Pensioner (_Eckrentner_):** 45 years of average contributions.
- **Replacement Rate Trends:**
  - Net replacement rate currently ~48%.
  - _Forecast (Pre-Reform):_ Would drop to <45% by 2040 due to the Sustainability Factor.
- **Contribution Rate Trends:**
  - Currently ~18.6%.
  - _Forecast:_ Breaking the 20% psychological barrier by 2028.

> **Speaker Notes:** The "Standard Pensioner" is a statistical artifact. [cite_start]Real careers are fractured (especially for women), similar to the "Gender Pension Gap" observed in Switzerland (32.8%)[cite: 591].

## Slide 10: Rentenpaket II – The Political Economy

- **The "Double Haltelinie" (Double Stop Line):**
  - Policy commitment: Replacement rate $\ge 48\%$ AND Contribution rate $\le 20\%$ (until 2025).
- **The New Deal (Rentenpaket II):**
  - Permanently fix level at 48% until 2039.
  - **Cost:** Deactivates the dampening effect of the Sustainability Factor.
  - **Financing:** Requires massive increase in tax subsidies (_Bundeszuschuss_).

> **Speaker Notes:** This represents a shift in intergenerational equity. By fixing the payout level, the variance is forced entirely onto the contributors (the young) or the tax budget.

---

## Slide 11: Paradigm Shift – _Generationenkapital_

- **Concept:** Sovereign Wealth Fund for Pensions.
- **Mechanism:**
  - Debt-financed arbitrage.
  - State issues bonds (Cost: ~$r_{bond}$) $\rightarrow$ Invests in Global Equities (Return: ~$r_{equity}$).
  - Assumes Equity Risk Premium ($r_{equity} > r_{bond}$) holds long-term.
- **Target:** €200bn fund volume mid-2030s to subsidize contribution rates.
- **Critique:**
  - Volume too small (needs ~€1 Trillion for full impact).
  - Governance risks (Political interference in asset allocation).

> [cite_start]**Speaker Notes:** This is Germany trying to mimic the Swiss 2nd Pillar [cite: 424] [cite_start]or the Japanese GPIF[cite: 915], but doing it _within_ the public sector balance sheet rather than individual accounts. It is a "Hedge Fund" strategy for the state.

## Slide 12: Future Forecast & Implicit Debt

- **Fiscal Crowding Out:**
  - Pension subsidies currently consume ~25-30% of the Federal Budget.
  - Limits fiscal space for infrastructure, defense, and education.
- **Implicit Pension Debt:**
  - The Present Value of promised future benefits $\approx$ 300% of GDP.
- **The "BoomeR" Effect:**
  - Voting power of the elderly creates resistance to actuarially necessary cuts (Median Voter Theorem).

> [cite_start]**Speaker Notes:** Refer to the Japanese situation where political polarity makes reforms hard[cite: 663]. Germany faces the same gridlock.

---

## Slide 13: Conclusion

- **Summary:**
  - Germany’s Points System is allocation-efficient but demographically vulnerable.
  - Recent reforms (_Rentenpaket II_) prioritize benefit adequacy over fiscal sustainability.
- **The Outlook:**
  - The move to _Generationenkapital_ is historically significant but mathematically insufficient.
  - The "Contract between Generations" is being renegotiated at the expense of the young.
- **Final Question:** Can productivity growth outpace the demographic drag?
